Selected Works

Studio and spatial-design projects: each one a question about material, light or people, answered in model, drawing and built prototype.

01

Core Studio · Exercise 1 · Paper tectonics

Flow

One sheet of paper, 25 by 25 centimetres, and three moves allowed: cuts, folds, creases. I divided the surface into a field of strips, letting their widths drift wider toward one edge, then pushed each strip to its limit, testing how many folds and splits a single plane could carry before it became something else. What emerged reads as topography: a folded terrain that ripples from tight pleats into open ground, conjured entirely from one flat sheet. Re-imagined at full scale, Flow becomes a canopy of shifting light and shadow.

02

Core Studio · Exercise 2 · Light & section

Eclipse

An inhabitable void carved beneath a sweeping terrain. Curved trapezoidal apertures peel open from the landform above, each one tuned to pour daylight into the wide gathering space at its base. As the sun tracks across the sky, the skylights cast slow, shifting crescents of light through the interior, an architectural eclipse that turns the section itself into a sundial. Worked through paired positive and negative models, the project treats emptiness as the primary building material.

03

Core Studio · Exercise 3 · Geometry & care

Gardenáf

A cottage built from the most austere vocabulary imaginable: one circle, one square, one equilateral triangle. The design began strictly in plan, then each figure was lifted into massing, the geometry hardening into form. Accessibility threads through every decision, step-free routes, gentle thresholds, clear sightlines, while the client's love of gardening becomes part of the architecture itself: rooms that open onto the soil, and green held at the centre of daily life. She's elderly, opinionated about her tomatoes, and this is very much her house.

04

Core Studio · Exercise 4 · Museum, Queen Elizabeth Walk, Singapore

Whatthe?! · Chaos in Contrast

A museum for human- and AI-made art, sited along Singapore's Queen Elizabeth Walk and conceived in dialogue with MoMA's curatorial ambition. The brief was simple to state and hard to resolve: put two practices in the same building without pretending they're the same thing. Whatthe?! stages human and machine as deliberate opposites, distinct wings, distinct temperaments, then dissolves the boundary, drawing visitors toward a shared central ground where the two meet, argue and occasionally agree. Undulating shells rise from the contoured site like that argument made solid.

Spatial Design · Design Thinking · Group project

Lattice Help You

★ Top Design Thinking Project, awarded across all modules, not just spatial design

A display system designed for the people architecture usually forgets: temporary market-stall owners. Our scissor-lattice structure folds flat in seconds, travels light, and springs open into a full display wall. Set-up and tear-down become a gesture instead of a chore. Built and tested at full scale, the project won top honours among every design-thinking project in the cohort, across all disciplines.

Spatial Studies

Three exercises in Spatial Design World, each one starting from the same premise: my room, my dog, and the gap between the two. Plan, isometric and axonometric, drawn in increasing resolution.

2.1

Spatial Design World · Exercise 1 · Floor plan

A Crappy Situation

The brief: draw a floor plan of a house and the life lived inside it. Mine came with a dog. The plan maps out what it's actually like to share a home with a pet, down to the small daily negotiations over territory, and the animation makes the case bluntly: my dog, my garden, and a mess I have to deal with on a regular basis. A crappy situation, literally.

2.2

Spatial Design World · Exercise 2 · Interior isometric

Two's a Crowd

Same dog, much smaller home. Exercise two re-ran the scenario in a tiny apartment room instead of a landed house, and the isometric works out the logistics in full: items hung on the wall and out of paw's reach, a step built so my dog can reach the bed, and a bed and toys of her own so she has somewhere that's hers. Two residents, one room, everything has to earn its place.

2.3

Spatial Design World · Exercise 3 · Exterior axonometric

You Are Here

The final drawing pulls back to the building itself: a full exterior axonometric, every detail and façade feature modelled and drafted. One window is picked out from the rest, shaded to mark it apart, because that's the one. Zoomed all the way out, this is still the same room from the previous two exercises, just seen from the outside looking in.

Collaborative Studio

A group project, carried from first sketch to a complete drawing set.

01

Spatial Design World · Exercise 4 · Group project · Residence

Lemme In House

A house built around one idea: seclusion and privacy, played out as a contrast between outside and in. From the street, the building reads as cold and uninviting, a closed face that gives nothing away. Cross the threshold and it flips, warm, open, generous with light. A raked roofline lifts the upper volume toward the view while a perforated screen wall filters the tropical light; inside, the section stages a continuous climb from a shaded ground floor to bright, open upper rooms. Carried as a team from first sketch to a full drawing set: storey plans, renders and a fully resolved exploded axonometric that lays the building open, layer by layer.

04

Material Studies

Structures & digital design fabrication: thinking with the hands, at the scale of the body. Brass, timber, steel and sailcloth.

Sail

Retractable canopy · Bamboo & fabric · Five-week group build

Five weeks, a bundle of bamboo poles and a single run of fabric. Searching for a way to hold a large canopy aloft with almost no material, we kept coming back to sailboats: rigging, mast and cloth carrying enormous surfaces in pure tension. That observation became the design, and the name. Sail fans open like canvas catching wind, shades the lawn beneath it, and folds away as quickly as it deploys.

Vine

Brass ring · Individual, lathe-turned

Cut from a solid brass cylinder on the lathe and finished by hand: an open band that ends in two veined leaves, as if a creeping vine had wound itself around the finger and decided to stay. Architecture at its smallest scale: precision, patience, ornament.

Drift

Stool · Group build, wood & steel

A triangular seat carved into soft curves: dynamic from across the room, quietly ergonomic the moment you sit. Hand-shaped timber meets a welded steel base in a study of how far comfort and tension can share one object.

Global Exchange

Design doesn’t stay on campus. Two journeys: one reading a city’s modernist past, one building its digital future.

Bangkok, Thailand · Modernism study trip

Reading Bangkok

A week spent treating the city as a living textbook, tracing how modernism arrived in Bangkok, bent to its climate and culture, and left its mark on shells, vaults and sun-broken façades. Standing inside the buildings we had only studied in lectures turned theory into instinct: every photograph here is a footnote made physical.

Surabaya, Indonesia · PCU × SUTD · Design-AI exchange

Surabaya, Second Place

A cross-university design-AI programme with Petra Christian University, working directly with a local koperasi, a clothes-making cooperative. We built a machine-learning-backed web platform that organises and tracks their inventory, clearing the bottleneck between stockroom and sewing table so production could grow. The exchange culminated in a hackathon, where my team took second place.

About

I’m Kaitlyn Cheng, an architecture student at the Singapore University of Technology and Design who believes the best ideas are tested with the hands.

My work moves freely across scales: a brass ring turned on a lathe, a stool you can actually sit on, a folding lattice that won top design-thinking project across every module in its cohort, a museum imagined for Singapore’s waterfront. Whether the medium is paper, timber, machine learning or light itself, I look for the moment a strict constraint turns into an unexpected freedom.
